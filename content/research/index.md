# Research

My research interests lie at the intersection of statistical learning, machine learning, and biomedical data science. I am particularly interested in developing interpretable statistical methods that extract reliable information from complex biological systems while maintaining scientific validity and interpretability.

My current work spans physiological signal analysis, computational biology, and health data science, with a common goal of developing data-driven approaches that improve our understanding of human health.

---

# Current Research

## Multimodal Physiological Data Analysis in Addiction and Cognitive Neuroscience

### Neurological Aspects of Addiction Lab  
University of Florida  
Advisor: Dr. Amanda Elton

I investigate physiological responses associated with addiction-related processes by analyzing heart rate variability (HRV) and cardiovascular signals collected during a double-blind, placebo-controlled neuroimaging study.

The project examines how physiological responses vary across experimental conditions and individual participants, requiring statistical approaches capable of modeling complex within-subject dependencies.

### Methods and Contributions

- Process physiological time-series data from pulse oximetry recordings
- Develop signal processing pipelines for heart rate extraction and quality control
- Analyze repeated-measures data using mixed-effects modeling
- Evaluate condition-specific physiological responses while accounting for participant variability
- Investigate temporal patterns in physiological responses during experimental blocks

### Statistical Methods

- Linear mixed-effects models
- Longitudinal/repeated-measures analysis
- Time-series preprocessing
- Physiological signal analysis
- Experimental design modeling

This work has strengthened my interest in statistical methods for understanding noisy biological systems where individual variation and measurement complexity are central challenges.

# Self-Supervised Learning for Biomedical Discovery

## Nettles Lab  
UF Scripps Institute  
Advisor: Dr. Kendall Nettles

I develop machine learning approaches for analyzing molecular dynamics datasets with the goal of predicting biomedical outcomes related to drug efficacy.

The project focuses on using representation learning methods to identify meaningful patterns from high-dimensional biological simulations.

### Methods and Contributions

- Implement self-supervised learning pipelines using autoencoder architectures
- Develop computational workflows for molecular dynamics datasets
- Explore learned representations for biomedical prediction tasks
- Investigate how machine learning models can extract biologically meaningful features from complex simulations

### Machine Learning Methods

- Deep learning
- Autoencoders
- Representation learning
- High-dimensional feature extraction
- Computational biology workflows

This experience has motivated my interest in developing machine learning methods that combine predictive performance with interpretability and scientific understanding.

### Research Focus

- Identifying patterns associated with preventative healthcare outcomes

# Research Philosophy

Across neuroscience, computational biology, and preventative medicine, my research interests are connected by a central question:

**How can statistical methodology help researchers extract trustworthy knowledge from increasingly complex biological data?**

I am particularly interested in developing methods that balance predictive accuracy with interpretability, allowing statistical models to become tools for scientific discovery rather than simply computational predictors.
