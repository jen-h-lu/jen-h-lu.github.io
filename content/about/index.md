# About Me

I am an undergraduate Statistics student at the University of Florida interested in developing interpretable statistical and machine learning methods for biomedical discovery. My work lies at the intersection of statistical learning, artificial intelligence, and computational biology, where I explore how quantitative methods can transform complex biological data into meaningful scientific insights.

My interest in statistics began with a simple question: **why?** Rather than viewing statistics as a collection of procedures, I became interested in understanding why methods work, when assumptions are justified, and how statistical reasoning can transform complex data into reliable scientific insights. Through experiences spanning neuroscience, computational biology, machine learning, and industry, I have developed a passion for creating statistical methods that are not only predictive, but also interpretable and scientifically meaningful.

## Research Philosophy

I believe the most impactful statistical methods are those that combine mathematical rigor with practical scientific relevance. In biomedical applications especially, models must do more than produce accurate predictions — they must quantify uncertainty, reveal meaningful patterns, and support researchers in making informed decisions. My goal is to develop statistical methodology that bridges theory and application, creating tools that help scientists better understand complex biological systems.
